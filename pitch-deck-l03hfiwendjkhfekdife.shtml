  • Top Coder

    Case Study

    • Launched in 2001.
    • Organized coding and design competitions growing a community of developers, designers and data scientists.
    • Sold IT services to corporate clients decomposing projects into modules, distributing the pieces for the community to build—while competing with each other.
    • Projects ended up being cheaper than average market rates.
    • By 2018 the community amassed 1.2 Million developers / designers / data scientists.

  • Advanced Algos

    Our Case

    • Launching in 2019.
    • Will organize a constellation of algorithmic trading competitions to grow a community of developers, traders and analysts.
    • Will take a cut on subscriptions sold to asset holders renting community-built, competition-winning bots to trade on their behalf.
    • Projecting to reach 1 million developers / traders / analysts by 2030.

  • Evolution

    Unique Selling Proposition

    By open sourcing bot’s source code after competitions end, the community forks the winners, improves them and puts the new generation to compete again, unleashing a relentless evolutionary process leading to the emergence of superalgos, capable of outperforming everyone at the markets.

  • Superalgos

    • Amateur / Open Source: Current intelligence is low as well as improvement rate.
    • Institutional Investors: High intelligence and linear improvement rate.
    • Advanced Algos: Starts with very low intelligence which increases exponentially.

  • ALGO

    Utility Token

    • Definition: The medium of exchange within the platform by which bots autonomously pay microfees for the services they consume and earn microfees for the services they offer.
    • Evolution: Trading bots modeled as Financial Beings, meaning that when they run out of ALGO tokens, they die.
    • Technology: An ERC-20 Token on Ethereum.

  • 10-Year Business Plan

    Years 1 to 3

    • We start with event-based competitions awarding cash prizes;
    • Leagues and championships follow, mapping popular sports organizations, awarding ALGO tokens to winners;
    • Ongoing tournaments attract audiences for whom we broadcast live;
    • This in turn attracts sponsors who offer hefty cash prizes for each major championship.

  • 10-Year Business Plan

    Years 4 to 6

    • National, continental championships and world cups follow;
    • As competitions evolve from amateur to professional teams, bots performance takes off;
    • Increasing performance attracts subscribers, willing to hire champions;
    • Technology is opened so that anyone can run unofficial tournaments (i.e.: schools, universities, investment funds, etc.)

  • 10-Year Business Plan

    Years 7 to 10

    • By the time we reach 1 Million developers, traders and analysts, we expand out of the crypto markets and into the broader electronic markets to compete in the big leagues.
    • Once big-league players notice our presence it will be too late, because:
      • Our one-million-members community runs on a zero-cost basis, incentivized by prizes paid by sponsors and the fees collected from renting their bots;
      • Our technology infrastructure costs are covered by running bots;
      • We dominate the algorithmic trading competitions space.
